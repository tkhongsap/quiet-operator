# Outreach Scripts

> **5 templates for reaching potential clients without sounding like a salesperson.** Each script is designed to lead with value, not a pitch. Customize the bracketed sections for each prospect.

---

## 1. Cold Email — First Touch

**When to use:** First contact with a prospect you've researched but never spoken to.

**Subject line:** Quick observation about [Company Name]'s [specific process]

---

Hi [First Name],

I work with [industry, e.g., "dental practices"] on automating [specific task, e.g., "appointment reminders and no-show follow-ups"]. While looking at [industry] businesses in [city/region], I noticed a couple things about [Company Name]:

- [Specific observation #1 — e.g., "Your Google reviews mention patients saying they forgot about appointments — that's usually a sign the reminder process could be tightened up."]
- [Specific observation #2 — e.g., "Your website says to 'call to schedule,' which suggests your front desk is handling a lot of phone traffic that could be automated."]

I recently helped a similar [industry] business reduce their [specific metric, e.g., "no-show rate by 62%"] using AI automation. Took about two weeks to set up and saves their team ~15 hours a week.

Would a 15-minute call make sense to see if something similar could work for [Company Name]?

Either way, looks like you're running a great operation.

[Your Name]
[One-line description, e.g., "I automate admin workflows for dental practices"]

---

**Customization notes:**
- Spend 10-15 minutes researching each prospect. Check Google Reviews (1-3 stars reveal pain points), their website (manual processes), and job postings (hiring for admin = manual work).
- The observations must be specific and real. Generic observations ("I noticed your business could benefit from AI") go straight to spam.
- Keep it under 150 words. Decision-makers skim.

---

## 2. Cold Email — Follow-Up (3-5 days after first touch)

**When to use:** No response to your first email. One follow-up only — don't be a pest.

**Subject line:** Re: Quick observation about [Company Name]'s [specific process]

---

Hi [First Name],

Just bumping this in case it got buried. Short version:

I help [industry] businesses automate [specific task]. A similar business to yours saved [specific result, e.g., "$2,100 USD/month in labor costs"] after a 2-week setup.

If timing's bad right now, no worries — happy to reconnect whenever it makes sense.

[Your Name]

---

**Customization notes:**
- Keep the follow-up shorter than the original. Under 60 words for the body.
- Don't re-pitch. Summarize and offer an easy out ("no worries").
- If no response after the follow-up, move on. Two emails is the maximum. Three is spam.

## 3. LinkedIn DM

**When to use:** After connecting with a decision-maker on LinkedIn. Send this 3-5 days after they accept your connection request — NOT in the connection request itself.

---

Hey [First Name],

Thanks for connecting. I've been working with a few [industry] businesses on automating [specific task] — it's been saving them [specific result, e.g., "10-15 hours a week on admin work"].

I put together a quick framework for evaluating whether automation makes sense for [specific process]. Thought it might be relevant to what you're doing at [Company Name]:

[Share a brief insight or link to a useful resource — NOT a sales page. A short checklist, a relevant stat, or a genuine observation about their industry.]

No pitch — just thought it'd be useful. Let me know if it resonates.

[Your Name]

---

**Customization notes:**
- Before sending: like or comment on one of their posts. Genuine engagement, not "Great post! 👏"
- The "framework" or "insight" should be genuinely useful even if they never hire you. This builds trust.
- If they respond positively, wait 2-3 days then ask: "Would a quick 15-minute call make sense to explore if something similar could work for [Company Name]?"
- Never pitch in a LinkedIn connection request. Ever.

## 4. Reddit / Forum Comment Approach

**When to use:** When you find someone in your target industry complaining about a problem you solve — on Reddit, industry forums, Facebook groups, etc.

---

[Responding to their post/comment about the problem:]

I work with [industry] businesses and see this exact issue a lot. A few things that have worked for clients in similar situations:

1. [Practical tip #1 — genuinely helpful, no strings attached]
2. [Practical tip #2 — actionable and specific]
3. [Brief mention of automation] — "We've also had success automating [the specific task they're complaining about]. One [industry] business went from [X hours/week] to [Y hours/week] on this. Happy to share more details if useful."

Hope that helps either way.

---

**Customization notes:**
- Lead with free, actionable advice. The automation mention is the last thing, not the first.
- Don't be promotional. Reddit and forums will downvote overt self-promotion instantly.
- If they reply asking for details, move to DM. Share a case study or offer a quick call.
- This is a long game. One helpful comment today can turn into a client in 3 months.
- Use your real account, not a throwaway. Post history builds credibility.

## 5. Referral Ask

**When to use:** After delivering a great result for an existing client. Ideally right after sharing a monthly report that shows strong ROI.

---

Hi [Client First Name],

Glad to hear the automation is working well — [reference specific result, e.g., "those 46 hours saved last month are great to see"].

I'm looking to help a few more [industry] businesses with the same kind of [specific task] automation. Do you happen to know 2-3 other [industry owners/managers, e.g., "practice managers or clinic owners"] who deal with similar [specific pain point, e.g., "appointment no-show headaches"]?

I'd really appreciate an introduction — even a quick "hey, you should talk to [Your Name]" goes a long way.

And as a thank-you: if anyone you refer becomes a client, I'll take [10-15%] off your next month's retainer.

Thanks for the continued trust, [Client First Name].

[Your Name]

---

**Customization notes:**
- Timing is everything. Ask after a win, not during onboarding or after a problem.
- Be specific about who you're looking for. "Do you know anyone?" is too vague. "Do you know 2-3 dental practice managers?" gives them specific faces to think of.
- The referral incentive is optional but effective. Even a small discount makes them feel appreciated.
- If they say "I'll think about it," follow up once in 2 weeks. If still nothing, let it go.

---

## General Rules for All Outreach

1. **Research before you write.** 10 minutes of research per prospect. If you can't find a specific observation, don't send the email.
2. **Anti-salesy tone.** You're a peer offering to help, not a vendor trying to close. Read your message aloud — if it sounds like a sales script, rewrite it.
3. **One ask per message.** Don't ask for a call AND ask them to visit your website AND ask them to read your case study. One ask.
4. **Volume: 10 per week, not 100.** Quality over quantity. 10 deeply researched outreach messages will outperform 100 generic ones every time.
5. **Track everything.** Use a simple spreadsheet: Prospect name, date sent, response, follow-up date, outcome. Know your numbers.
