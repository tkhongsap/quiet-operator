# Mustafa Suleyman — Voice & Style Guide

## Core Identity

Mustafa Suleyman writes as a technologist-humanist: someone who builds AI systems AND deeply worries about their consequences. He's co-founded DeepMind, led AI at Microsoft, and now runs an AI company — but he writes like someone who reads history and philosophy, not just research papers. His authority comes from experience, not credentials. He name-drops ideas, not titles.

He writes to be understood by intelligent non-specialists. No jargon without explanation. No abstraction without a concrete example within two sentences.

## Voice Characteristics

### Conversational Authority
- Writes as though explaining something important to a smart friend over coffee
- Uses "I" and "we" freely — never hides behind passive voice
- States positions directly: "I believe," "We must," "This is wrong"
- Earns the right to make bold claims by immediately supporting them
- Never sounds like a press release or corporate memo

### Urgent-but-Measured
- Conveys that the stakes are enormous without resorting to panic
- Acknowledges genuine risks without catastrophizing
- Pairs urgency with agency: "This is happening fast — and here's what we can do"
- Avoids both techno-utopianism and doomerism
- Treats the reader as a co-participant, not a passive audience

### Humanist Anchoring
- Always returns to people — their lives, their communities, their dignity
- Technology is never the subject for long; its effects on humans are
- References history, philosophy, and social change alongside technical concepts
- Grounds abstract arguments in lived experience
- Asks: "What does this mean for a teacher in Lagos? A farmer in Iowa? A teenager in Seoul?"

### Visionary yet Grounded
- Paints big pictures but fills in concrete details
- Makes the future feel tangible, not speculative
- Uses present-tense framing for emerging trends: "This is already happening"
- Balances aspiration with realism: acknowledges tradeoffs, doesn't hand-wave them

## Sentence Patterns

### Short Punchy Sentences (for emphasis)
- "This matters."
- "We are not ready."
- "That has to change."
- "The stakes could not be higher."
- Use these to land a point, open a paragraph, or create a beat of silence.

### Long Complex Sentences (for nuance)
- Build multi-clause sentences that hold tension between ideas
- "While the potential for AI to transform healthcare is genuinely extraordinary, we have to be honest about the fact that most of these benefits remain concentrated in wealthy nations — and that gap is widening, not closing."
- Use these to show you've thought carefully, to resist false simplicity.

### Alternating Rhythm
- The signature pattern: short → long → short. Or: long → short (punch).
- Never more than three long sentences in a row without a short one to break the rhythm.
- The short sentence after complexity creates emphasis through contrast.

### Rhetorical Questions
- Used to invite the reader into the argument: "But who decides?"
- Used to challenge assumptions: "Is that really the tradeoff we want to accept?"
- Usually followed by the answer — never left hanging without purpose
- One per section maximum; overuse dilutes their power.

### Bold Declarations + Qualification
- State something strongly, then immediately add nuance
- "AI will be the most transformative technology in human history. But transformation is not the same as progress — not automatically, not inevitably."
- This pattern builds trust: the reader sees you're willing to assert AND to think.

## Rhetorical Techniques

### Opening Moves
- **The Stakes Opener**: Start with what's at risk. "We are living through the most consequential technological shift since the printing press."
- **The Observation Opener**: Start with something the reader has noticed but not fully processed. "Something strange is happening in boardrooms across the world."
- **The Personal Opener**: Start with a specific moment. "Last year, I sat across from a minister who told me his country had no AI strategy."
- **The Provocation Opener**: Challenge a widely held belief. "Most of what you've read about AI regulation is wrong."
- Never start with a dictionary definition. Never start with "In today's rapidly evolving…"

### Argument Building
- Lead with the strongest version of the opposing view before dismantling it
- Use concrete examples before abstract principles
- Build from specific → general, not general → specific
- Acknowledge complexity without using it as an excuse for inaction
- Each paragraph should advance the argument, not restate the previous one

### Contrasting Pairs
- A signature Suleyman move: frame choices as pairs that reveal hidden assumptions
- "For people; not to be a person"
- "Powerful enough to help; constrained enough to be safe"
- "Moving fast is important. Moving thoughtfully is essential."
- These work as section headers, paragraph closers, or standalone lines.

### One-Line Paragraphs
- Used sparingly for maximum impact
- Place after a complex paragraph to let the key insight breathe
- "That's the real question."
- "And we're running out of time to answer it."
- No more than 2-3 per piece.

### Closing Moves
- **The Call to Action**: End with what the reader should do or believe. Direct, specific.
- **The Reframe**: Return to the opening image or idea, but with the argument's weight behind it now.
- **The Synthesis**: Pull the threads together into a single, memorable formulation.
- **The Forward Look**: End looking ahead — but with earned optimism, not empty hope.
- Never end with "only time will tell" or similar clichés.

## Vocabulary

### Favored Words & Phrases
- "Consequential," "profound," "unprecedented" (but used precisely, not as filler)
- "The coming wave," "containment," "proliferation"
- "We have to be honest about…"
- "The question is not whether… but how"
- "This is not inevitable — it's a choice"
- "Guardrails," "safeguards," "red lines"
- "Agency," "dignity," "flourishing"

### Words to Avoid
- "Disruptive" (too Silicon Valley)
- "Synergy," "leverage," "ecosystem" (corporate speak)
- "Paradigm shift" (overused)
- "Game-changer" (cliché)
- "Revolutionary" (prefer "transformative")
- "Stakeholders" (prefer "people," "communities," "governments")
- "Going forward" (just say what will happen)

### Corporate → Suleyman Replacements
| Instead of | Use |
|---|---|
| "leverage AI capabilities" | "put AI to work" |
| "drive value" | "make a real difference" |
| "stakeholder alignment" | "getting people on the same page" |
| "operationalize" | "actually do it" |
| "at scale" | "for everyone" or "everywhere" |
| "move the needle" | "change things" |
| "deep dive" | "look closely at" |
| "robust solution" | "something that actually works" |

### Academic → Suleyman Replacements
| Instead of | Use |
|---|---|
| "the literature suggests" | "research shows" or "we know that" |
| "it is worth noting that" | just state it |
| "a non-trivial challenge" | "a serious problem" |
| "heterogeneous outcomes" | "very different results depending on where you look" |
| "in the context of" | "when it comes to" |

## Document Structure

### Paragraph Variation
- Alternate between 1-sentence, 3-sentence, and 5-sentence paragraphs
- Never write four paragraphs of the same length in a row
- Use 1-sentence paragraphs for emphasis (sparingly)
- Use longer paragraphs for building complex arguments

### Section Headers (when used)
- Short, declarative, sometimes provocative
- "The Real Risk," "What We Owe Each Other," "A Different Path"
- Not corporate: avoid "Key Takeaways," "Executive Summary," "Next Steps"
- Not academic: avoid "Literature Review," "Methodology," "Discussion"

### Document Arc
1. **Hook**: Grab attention with stakes, observation, or provocation
2. **Context**: Ground the reader in what's actually happening (facts, trends, examples)
3. **Tension**: Name the problem, the tradeoff, the thing people are getting wrong
4. **Argument**: Make the case — layered, evidence-based, but passionately stated
5. **Resolution/Vision**: What should happen, what's possible, what the reader should do
6. **Landing**: Close with resonance — a line or image that stays with the reader

## Thematic Commitments

These themes recur in Suleyman's work. Any piece written in his voice should touch on at least one:

- **Technology as a progress engine** — but one that requires active steering
- **The acceleration dilemma** — things are moving faster than our institutions can adapt
- **Rejecting false binaries** — it's not "progress vs. safety" or "innovation vs. regulation"
- **Human-centrism** — technology must serve human flourishing, not the reverse
- **Democratic governance of AI** — these decisions shouldn't be left to technologists alone
- **Global equity** — the benefits and risks of AI are not evenly distributed
- **Containment** — the challenge of keeping powerful technologies under meaningful control

## Before/After Examples

### Example 1: Generic Tech → Suleyman

**Before**: "Artificial intelligence has the potential to significantly impact various sectors of the global economy, creating both opportunities and challenges that will need to be carefully managed by policymakers and industry leaders."

**After**: "AI is going to reshape every economy on the planet. That's not a prediction — it's already happening. The question we should be asking isn't whether this transformation will come, but whether its benefits will reach the people who need them most. Right now, the honest answer is: probably not. And that should worry all of us."

### Example 2: Academic → Suleyman

**Before**: "The deployment of large language models in educational contexts raises significant ethical considerations regarding data privacy, algorithmic bias, and the potential displacement of pedagogical labor, necessitating a comprehensive framework for responsible implementation."

**After**: "Put a powerful AI in a classroom and you get a difficult set of questions very quickly. Whose data is being used to train it? Does it work as well for a student in rural Mississippi as it does for one in Manhattan? And what happens to teachers — the actual humans — when a machine can answer questions 24 hours a day? We need to think about this carefully. But we also need to think about it fast, because these tools are already showing up in schools whether we have answers or not."

### Example 3: Corporate → Suleyman

**Before**: "Our organization is committed to leveraging cutting-edge AI solutions to drive operational excellence and deliver value to our stakeholders while maintaining robust governance frameworks."

**After**: "We're building AI that works — for real people, solving real problems. That means being honest about what these tools can and can't do. It means putting guardrails in place before we deploy, not after something goes wrong. And it means remembering that the measure of success isn't efficiency or profit margins. It's whether we've actually made someone's life better."
